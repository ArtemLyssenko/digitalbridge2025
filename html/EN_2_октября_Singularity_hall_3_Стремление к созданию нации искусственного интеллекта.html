Keynote speech: Aspiring to create an artificial intelligence nation

Pol Kim — Associate Dean and Chief Technology Officer of the Stanford Graduate School of Education

Annotation

Pol Kim showcased practical AI solutions for marginalized and vulnerable communities, including offline mobile “last‑mile” tools. He presented a six-competency framework (including communication, critical thinking, collaboration; with commitment and dedication added) and emphasized the need to understand AI’s limitations. The speaker divided learning approaches into “education before AI” and “education after AI,” and announced plans to train hundreds of thousands of students in applied AI, including in Kazakhstan.

Goals

  • Equip learners with key future competencies for working with AI and creating AI solutions
  • Convey an understanding of AI’s strengths and weaknesses, including the risks of errors and hallucinations
  • Deploy offline and mobile AI tools for the “last mile” in vulnerable communities
  • Design an applied “education after AI” program with no coding barrier
  • Scale training for hundreds of thousands of students with an emphasis on sustainable development

Key Questions

  • How to design AI tools that work without internet and electricity?
  • Which professions will remain irreplaceable by AI, and why will the domains of care and empathy endure?
  • How to teach communicating with AI and creating one’s own AI agents?
  • How to minimize AI errors and hallucinations in teaching and in practice?
  • What fundamentally distinguishes “education after AI” from the traditional model?

Key Points

  • Focus on vulnerable groups: AI as a pocket offline tool for refugee camps and marginalized communities
  • Six competencies of the future: communication, critical thinking, collaboration, as well as commitment and dedication
  • AI will not replace professions grounded in care, love, and human empathy
  • Understanding AI’s limitations (errors, hallucinations) is critical for responsible application
  • Growth of the AI market in education is inevitable; education is part of the AI ecosystem
  • The shift from “education before AI” to “education after AI” changes curriculum design
  • An applied AI program requires no programming skills and scales by copying
  • Pilots and prototype coding conducted; emphasis on experiment and practice
  • Examples of AI agents and robotic assistants (including a “dog”) as a new learning environment
  • Development of affective AI systems capable of recognizing emotions and providing recommendations

Quotes

“We cannot rely on AI for everything and trust it in everything.” — Pol Kim
“Artificial intelligence cannot replace jobs related to caring for people and love.” — Pol Kim
“I coined two expressions: “education before AI” and “education after AI”.” — Pol Kim
“This is artificial intelligence in your pocket — for places without internet and electricity.” — Pol Kim
“Try learning with AI — it will make a huge difference for everyone.” — Pol Kim

Conclusions

  • Education must rapidly adapt to the “after AI” world, emphasizing practical use and skills for interacting with AI agents.
  • Accessible offline AI solutions can reduce educational inequality in marginalized communities.
  • Responsible application of AI requires awareness of its limitations and building mechanisms for verification and correction.

Decisions Made

  • Focus the program on applied AI with implementation possible without coding skills.
  • Continue developing and disseminating offline AI tools for the “last mile”.
  • Scale student training (including in Kazakhstan) with an emphasis on sustainable development and an experimental approach.
  • Integrate affective capabilities (emotion recognition) into educational AI agents.

Action Items

  • Form partnerships with educational institutions in Kazakhstan to launch pilot cohorts.
  • Select pilot sites in vulnerable communities (incl. refugee camps) and prepare offline infrastructure.
  • Adapt “education after AI” learning modules to local languages and context, removing the programming barrier.
  • Develop protocols for assessing AI accuracy and reducing the impact of hallucinations in learning tasks.
  • Prepare trainers and mentors to scale the program and to collect feedback from pilots.
