The Power of Choice: Human Development at the Crossroads of Artificial Intelligence

Katarzhina Vavernya — UNDP Representative in Kazakhstan, moderator
Pedro Konseysao — Director of the Human Development Report Office (HDRO), UNDP, New York (online)
Gulzat Kobenova — Vice Minister of Science and Higher Education of the Republic of Kazakhstan
Yekaterina Smyshlyaeva — Member of the Mazhilis of the Parliament of the Republic of Kazakhstan
Dmitriy Mun — Vice Minister of Artificial Intelligence and Digital Development of the Republic of Kazakhstan
Von-San Cha — Head of the Digital Strategy Department of the Small Business and Market Service of the Ministry of SMEs and Startups of the Republic of Korea

Annotation

The session presented key findings of the 2025 Human Development Report on the role of AI: despite historically high levels of human development, progress is slowing and gaps are widening. The complementary "human+AI" model, risks of bias, and mental health were discussed. Kazakhstan showcased systemic steps — integrating AI into education, research infrastructure, pilots of government AI agents, and a new AI law; Korea shared its national strategy and infrastructure investments. The common call is for human-centered and inclusive AI deployment so that no one is left behind.

Goals

  • Present the findings of the 2025 Human Development Report on the impact of AI on education, healthcare, and public administration
  • Assess AI risks and opportunities with an emphasis on complementarity and reducing inequality
  • Share Kazakhstan's national practices in workforce development, infrastructure, regulation, and AI applications
  • Discuss the Republic of Korea's international experience in implementing the national AI strategy
  • Agree on benchmarks for next steps toward human-centered and safe AI deployment

Key Questions

  • How can we reconcile accelerating AI innovation with the 'human at the center' principle and prioritizing augmentation rather than replacement?
  • What mechanisms are needed to reduce AI model bias and the growth of global inequality?
  • How should AI regulation be designed in the absence of strict international standards — where is the line between local norms and global coordination?
  • How can we protect personal data, content rights, and counter deepfakes amid mass AI deployment?
  • What training and reskilling models will ensure broad digital literacy and demand for new professions?

Key Points

  • HDR‑2025 records a historically high level of human development, but the slowest progress in recent years and a widening gap between countries.
  • Global surveys: in countries with low HDI there are high expectations for active AI use; users more often expect AI to augment labor than to fully automate it.
  • Generative models carry cultural and geographic data biases (greater correlation with the views of high‑HDI countries), which is important to account for in automation.
  • AI can amplify creativity and science (the AlphaGo case — a surge in players' mastery; applications for predicting protein structures and drug discovery).
  • A worrying trend in youth mental health coincides with the widespread adoption of digital technologies — this requires close study.
  • Kazakhstan has integrated AI into the curricula of 95 universities; 30 universities run 38 specialized programs; 54% of faculty have completed AI upskilling.
  • Infrastructure initiatives launched: AI centers and labs, an academic supercomputer cluster is being formed and a unified access network for research.
  • Large‑scale training is underway: 440 thousand students have completed courses; the plan is to train 1 million citizens; the national AI platform and pilots of 50 AI agents for government agencies have been deployed.
  • Legal regulation in the Republic of Kazakhstan: an adopted AI law with a risk‑based approach, bans on remote biometrics without consent, emotion recognition, and discriminatory scoring; copyright — training is permissible, a machine‑readable opt‑out is introduced, and creative prompts may be recognized as protectable objects.
  • Experience of the Republic of Korea: a national AI 'control tower', 100 trillion won for infrastructure (including 50 thousand GPUs), training 1 million specialists, priorities in SMEs and public services; emphasis on regional pilots (e.g., «Цифровая Кызылорда»).

Conclusions

  • AI can accelerate human development with a human‑centered, complementary approach and by addressing risks of bias and privacy.
  • In the absence of strict international norms, high‑quality national regulation, infrastructure, and mass workforce training play a key role.
  • Inclusiveness is a critical success criterion: the benefits of AI must reach regions, SMEs, and vulnerable groups so as not to exacerbate inequality.

Decisions Made

  • Support a focus on the complementary "human+AI" model and the human‑centered principle reflected in HDR‑2025.
  • Hold an open workshop on the draft «Цифровой кодекс» (including AI and data provisions) for the professional community.
  • Scale up pilots of the national AI platform and AI agents in government agencies based on current results.
  • Strengthen regional pilots and cooperation with the Republic of Korea (including «Цифровая Кызылорда») to support SMEs and local communities.

Action Items

  • Publish a localized HDR‑2025 digest for government bodies, universities, and businesses with practical recommendations.
  • Develop methodological guidelines for human‑centered AI deployment in public services (including risk assessment and data quality).
  • Launch a registry of machine‑readable opt‑outs for authors on the use of their content in training AI models, and an enforcement mechanism.
  • Expand the academic supercomputer cluster and provide network access to all universities for AI research.
  • Continue the large‑scale training program (students, civil servants, SMEs) with a focus on practical creation and use of AI agents.
  • Prepare and implement standards to counter deepfakes and protect personal data in educational and government organizations.
  • Deploy monitoring of the impact of digital technologies on youth mental health and prevention measures at the education system level.
