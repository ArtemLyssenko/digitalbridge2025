Why venture is looking at Latin America: fintech, regulation, and opportunities for growth

Михаил Батарев — venture investor, managing his own fund (global fintech, Latin America)

Annotation

The speaker explained why the focus of global venture is shifting to Latin America: the world’s VC dependence on the dollar and U.S. markets, the downturn in China/India/Europe, and attractive asset valuations in LatAm. The main driver of the region is fintech built on a mature regulatory framework (Pix, open banking) and legal stablecoins, with El Salvador playing a critical on/off-ramp role. Demographic and economic factors are shown (young urban population, nearshoring, IT arbitrage), as well as risks (inflation, politics, brain drain). Noted: AI is used in applied ways, but sovereign foundation models in the region are unlikely in the coming years.

Goals

  • Show why Latin America is a priority region for venture investments after the U.S.
  • Break down the architecture of the LatAm fintech ecosystem: payment rails, open banking, the role of crypto and stablecoins
  • Compare macro trends: VC dependence on the U.S. and the decline in activity in China, India, and Europe
  • Outline the key business risks in the region and ways to account for them
  • Assess the realism of developing sovereign AI in Latin America

Key Questions

  • Why is global venture structurally tied to the dollar and U.S. exchanges, and how does this change the regional map?
  • What factors make Latin America more attractive to investors compared to Europe, China, and India?
  • What role do Pix, local cards, open banking, and stablecoins play in the growth of fintech in the region?
  • What risks do non-local teams face (inflation, politics, talent migration), and how can they be hedged?
  • Is sovereign AI possible in Latin America in the next 3–5 years, and what conditions are necessary for this?

Key Points

  • Global VC is systemically tied to the U.S. and the dollar; the withdrawal of American capital crashes local markets (cases of China, India, and Europe since 2022).
  • Latin America is the main regional focus for American investors, with more attractive valuations and the potential to enter the U.S. market.
  • Two locomotives — Brazil (a self-contained and mature ecosystem, several instant payment systems, 4 local card schemes) and Mexico; Peru, Colombia, and Argentina are coming up.
  • Demographics and economy: 600+ million population, a young urban audience, rapid technology adoption; nearshoring is moving production to Mexico, strengthening cash flows and capabilities.
  • IT arbitrage: LatAm has become a talent source for the U.S. in a convenient time zone, with significantly lower salaries and a high quality of life in capital metro areas.
  • Financial inclusion is low: the top banks in Mexico are foreign; access to accounts and credit is limited, creating a window for fintech startups.
  • The legality of stablecoins and cryptocurrencies in the region and El Salvador’s role as an on/off-ramp to the 'real dollar' accelerate cross-border payments (often over crypto rails without SWIFT).
  • Open banking and the principle of ownership rights over a user’s financial data encourage custom products (for example, usage-based insurance) and service competition.
  • Regulators of major economies are flexible and effective: over 5 years, only isolated bank failures; Pix is scaling beyond Brazil, and B2B payments are growing rapidly.
  • AI in LatAm is used in applied ways, but creating sovereign foundation models in the next 5 years is unlikely due to requirements for capital, talent, and electricity.

Quotes

Conclusions

  • Latin America has become the second most important source of quality venture deals after the U.S., with a clear predominance of fintech.
  • The region’s regulatory and payment infrastructure (Pix, open banking, stablecoins) creates unique conditions for rapid scaling of financial services.
  • Despite inflation and political risks, the return/risk profile in LatAm in the current cycle surpasses Europe, China, and India; sovereign AI in the region is unlikely in the near term.

Decisions Made

  • Focus analysis and deal flow on Latin American fintech with an emphasis on Brazil and Mexico.
  • Evaluate deals through the lens of potential entry into the U.S. market and linkage to dollar liquidity.
  • Use crypto and stablecoin rails (via El Salvador as an on/off-ramp) for cross-border fintech solutions.
  • Do not invest in projects to create 'sovereign' AI foundation models in the region; limit to applied AI.
  • Build partnerships with local regulators and payment operators (for example, the Pix ecosystem) to speed up product launches.
