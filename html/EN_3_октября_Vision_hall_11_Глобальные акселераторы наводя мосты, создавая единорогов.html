Panel Session: Global Accelerators: Building Bridges, Creating Unicorns

Mukhammad Makhmudov — Director, Plug and Play Uzbekistan
Olga Skorokhodova — Head of Government Relations and Public Policy for Emerging Markets at Google
Pedro Santos Vieira — Managing Partner, 500 Global
Muhannad Tashlak — Chief Investment Officer, Alchemist Qatar
Shams Imran — Executive Director of the Accelerate Prosperity initiative

Annotation

The panel discussed the transformation of accelerators from mentorship/funding tools into ecosystem architects working with governments and corporations. Emphasis was placed on scaling strategies from frontier markets without premature expansion, early AI integration, and cloud-first/free cross-border data policies. It examined mechanisms for attracting capital and building founder resilience (simulations, mental health) and pathways for developing deep tech through university and international partnerships.

Goals

  • Define how next-generation accelerators should work with government and corporations to grow the ecosystem.
  • Formulate policy (cloud-first, cross-border data flows) that unlocks venture capital in Central Asia.
  • Identify proven internationalization strategies and avoid premature scaling.
  • Outline an approach to developing deep tech in the region through the diaspora, universities, and industry partnerships.
  • Embed AI and resilience/psychological support practices into acceleration programs.

Key Questions

  • How have the roles and models of accelerators changed in recent years, and what is an “Accelerator 2.0”?
  • Which government policies are critical for the globalization of startups (cloud-first, cross-border data, funds of funds)?
  • How can startups from frontier markets scale without premature expansion into the US/EU, and where should they validate PMF?
  • Which practices help attract international capital (storytelling, relationship “lines,” local traction)?
  • How do you build founder resilience in volatile markets (normalizing failure, simulation labs, mental health)?

Key Points

  • Accelerators have evolved into platforms that reshape the landscape: they work with regulators, corporations, and infrastructure, not just mentorship/checks.
  • Early AI integration and moving to the cloud are mandatory for global scale; the region needs an AI-first strategy.
  • Key policies: the cloud-first principle and easing cross-border data flows; without this it is hard to scale from Central Asia.
  • Governments are increasing their role via funds of funds, sector priorities, and partnerships (Qatar as an example) — support without excessive control.
  • The main internationalization mistake is premature scaling; first achieve PMF and traction in regional markets.
  • Choosing an accelerator is choosing people and a network: who is inside the program and what bridges to target markets it provides matter.
  • Deep tech in the region requires linkage with universities and the diaspora, industry accelerators, and ecosystem programs (example: Alchemist + University of Chicago).
  • Attracting capital: strong storytelling, local validation, and building relationship “lines” with investors rather than one-off “points.”
  • Founder resilience is a strategic factor: normalizing failure, crisis simulations in programs, and attention to mental health.

Quotes

“Investors invest in lines, not points.” — Pedro Santos Vieira
“We need to normalize that failure is part of learning.” — Shams Imran
“You need to make all your decisions based on data — use AI to analyze that data.” — Muhannad Tashlak

Conclusions

  • A consensus emerged: accelerators should act as ecosystem system integrators together with governments and corporations.
  • Early AI and cloud infrastructure are essential for global growth; regulatory barriers around data require priority removal.
  • Scaling should be based on local/regional validation and a support network, avoiding early entry into complex markets.
  • Founder resilience is a competitive advantage of frontier markets; accelerators should implement simulations and mental health support.

Decisions Made

  • Support the development of regional programs (e.g., Silk Way) with enhanced modules on AI, storytelling, and working with international investors.
  • Develop expert recommendations for Central Asian governments on implementing cloud-first policy and easing cross-border data flows.
  • Include resilience simulation labs and a founder mental health component in acceleration tracks.
  • Expand partnerships with industry and international hubs (Alchemist, 500 Eurasia, Astana Hub) to build bridges to target markets and develop deep tech.

Action Items

  • Prepare a policy brief for Central Asian regulators on cloud-first and cross-border data, incorporating Qatar’s practices.
  • Create a map of regional and international investors/accelerators and establish relationship “line” channels for founders.
  • Launch pilot modules “Storytelling & Fundraising” and “AI-by-default” in regional accelerators.
  • Organize a roadshow/founder delegations to Doha, Astana, and Tashkent with pre-arranged B2B meetings.
  • Deploy simulation labs for crisis scenarios in acceleration programs (cash shortfalls, recession, regulatory risks).
  • Create an initiative to engage the deep tech diaspora (mentorship, co-founding, R&D nodes with universities).
