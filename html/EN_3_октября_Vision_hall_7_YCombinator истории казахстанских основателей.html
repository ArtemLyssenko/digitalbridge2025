Panel session: Y Combinator: stories of Kazakhstani founders

Annotation

The panel focused on the practical path into Y Combinator: from the application and the tough 10-minute interview to repeat submissions and the role of traction. The speakers shared different trajectories — from biotech and developer tools to AI call centers — and sales/validation tactics (personal installs, early payments). They discussed networking (warm intros, cold messages), fundraising, and the specifics of YC partners. The session ended with a Q&A and an agreement to have a short networking meetup at the Eurasian Hub booth.

Goals

  • Show real scenarios of getting into Y Combinator and passing the interview
  • Provide practical tips on the application, validation, and building traction
  • Give the audience an understanding of how to build sales and product in parallel
  • Highlight the role of networking and referrals for applying and fundraising
  • Connect participants with founders for follow-up consultations

Key Questions

  • What are the key success factors in a startup and what does YC look for in a team?
  • How best to show the uniqueness of the project in the YC application and how to substantiate it?
  • How to build networking effectively and get feedback on the application from YC alumni?
  • What to focus on first: product or sales to customers?
  • How to compete with strong teams in the market (for example, Cursor) and how to differentiate?

Key Points

  • The YC application must be exceptionally clear and concise; long answers are a red flag that you don’t understand your business.
  • The YC interview is 10 minutes of tough, rapid questions; they test the depth of understanding of the problem, customer, and plan.
  • Repeat submissions are normal: traction and progress between applications significantly increase your chances.
  • Validation through paying customers even for small amounts (e.g., $100–$200) is better than any LOIs.
  • Active offline sales (“Colllison install”): go to offices, install the product, gather instant feedback.
  • A network is built with a mix of warm intros and cold messages; persistent selling of yourself and the product matters.
  • Biotech requires large budgets and experiments; sometimes it’s more strategic to start with software and services for revenue.
  • YC partners vary in style (strict and friendly); prepare for each interview precisely, with numbers and a demo.
  • Location and relocation: YC expects full engagement and readiness to work from San Francisco during the batch.
  • The Kazakhstani ecosystem at YC is growing: there have already been six startups, four are represented on the panel.

Conclusions

  • YC values clarity of thought, proven demand, and the willingness to iterate quickly; formal signs of a “beautiful” application are secondary.
  • Traction and real customers outweigh words and LOIs; even a small payment is a meaningful signal.
  • A strong network and bold direct actions (offline installs, cold messages) accelerate sales and your chances at YC.
  • For capital-intensive industries, it’s strategic to combine R&D with software/services to grow revenue and investor trust.

Action Items

  • Participants: prepare a concise and clear YC application; remove jargon and leave only facts and metrics.
  • Prepare a demo and practice the 10-minute interview with mock sessions, including tough questions about the market and customers.
  • Find your first paying customers (even for small amounts) and record retention/value metrics.
  • Activate your network: ask for warm intros to YC alumni and partners; in parallel, do high-quality cold outreach.
  • Organizers/speakers: compile and send a list of useful materials about YC (essays, videos, checklists).
  • Founders: conduct “offline installs” at target customers for accelerated feedback and adoption.
  • Teams in capital-intensive fields: think through a software component/services for early revenue and investor de-risking.
