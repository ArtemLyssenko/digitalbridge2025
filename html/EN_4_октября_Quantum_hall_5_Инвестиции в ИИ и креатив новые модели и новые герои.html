AI, investments, and the creative industries: from hype to sustainable value

Andrey Zuzin — Founder of Erin Tech Venture Capital (formerly VTB VC)
Arijit Bhattacharya — Founder of Virtual Infocom and the investment council of Coinnovate Ventures
Timur Bairov — CEO of Фридом Медиа
Dias Ermaganbetov — Partner at Big Sky Capital (replacing Zhana Kosybaeva)
Nastya Goncharova — Co-founder of the governing committee of Creative Central Asia Network (CCAN)

Annotation

The panel discussed three levels of AI application and the gap between hype and real value. Investors emphasized the risk of chasing B2C hype and the need to invest in teams capable of combining creativity and technology. The creative industries need 'patient' impact capital and AI tools as an amplifier rather than a replacement for the author. The key challenge is to preserve the cultural code and cultivate cross-disciplinary teams in Kazakhstan.

Goals

  • Identify sustainable AI monetization models in the creative industries beyond the hype
  • Assess the readiness of Kazakhstan's ecosystem for deep tech projects and the conditions for repeatable success
  • Formulate approaches to protecting the cultural code and intellectual property when using AI
  • Define an impact-investing framework ('patient capital') for SMEs in the creative sector
  • Outline steps to develop cross-disciplinary teams (creative + data/engineering)

Key Questions

  • How to turn AI from a source of 'work slob' into a source of real value and revenue?
  • How risky is B2C in AI compared to B2B SaaS, and how to manage these risks?
  • How to preserve originality and cultural identity when generating content with AI models trained on external data?
  • What role do education, critical thinking, and 'prompt engineering' play in the quality of AI outputs?
  • What mechanisms will attract 'patient' impact capital to creative SMEs in Central Asia?

Key Points

  • AI as a technological field is not new; the leap is tied to generative models and LLMs, but the effect is often overestimated for creativity and underestimated for utilitarian tasks (dubbing, localization).
  • There is a 'democratic' layer of AI tools, a corporate layer with human-in-the-loop, and a layer of debates about originality vs. compilation (example with Miyazaki's style).
  • Stanford criticizes inefficient use of AI ('work slob'), which leads to costs without knowledge gains.
  • Music industry: with ~$43 billion in annual turnover, artists receive about $5 billion — potential to optimize distribution chains via AI/IT.
  • Success in the creative industries depends on hitting the cultural 'mindset'; AI is still weak in screenwriting and deep dramaturgy.
  • The key shortage is cross-disciplinary teams (screenwriter + data scientist); 'prompt engineers' are prone to burnout.
  • Investment focus is shifting from pure technology to teams and products with validated demand; avoid chasing hype.
  • The creative industries are 90% microbusinesses and require impact investments with a patient horizon.
  • Kazakhstan needs local heroes (10–15 over 5 years) and its own datasets/infrastructure that reflect the cultural code.
  • AI is a tool: from AI teachers for remote regions to avatar-actors with legal protection of likeness and monetization.

Conclusions

  • Betting on cross-disciplinary teams and cultural context yields more sustainable results than racing after new AI 'features'.
  • The impact approach and 'patient' capital fit the dynamics of SMEs in the creative industries better than classic venture capital.
  • Kazakhstan needs its own data and tooling infrastructure (including for localization and model training) to scale the local cultural code.
  • AI is effective as an amplifier and infrastructure layer (dubbing, educational avatars, optimization of value chains), but does not replace the author's intent.

Decisions Made

  • Shift investment focus from pure AI technologies to strong interdisciplinary teams and products.
  • Freedom Media is open to experiments and collaborations with local creators and AI projects for content and localization.
  • Big Sky Capital will continue developing its own LLM platform for deal scoring and plans to offer it to other investors.
  • It is recommended to build local datasets and human-in-the-loop practices to preserve cultural identity.
  • Support impact financing instruments for creative SMEs (small checks, pilots, micro-funds).

Action Items

  • Launch interdisciplinary labs (screenwriters + data engineers) and an accelerator focused on the cultural code.
  • Collect and license local datasets (language, folklore, visual archives) for model fine-tuning and dubbing.
  • Develop a guideline on copyright and AI ethics (styles, avatars, right to likeness) for the creative ecosystem.
  • Hold a series of workshops on prompt engineering and critical thinking for creators and producers.
  • Pilot AI avatar-teachers for remote regions with human-in-the-loop curators.
  • Freedom Media to announce an open call for AI experiments (dubbing into Kazakh, generative trailers, stand-up/long-form).
  • Big Sky Capital to prepare beta access to its LLM platform for partner VCs and angel communities.
