International Competitions in Programming and Artificial Intelligence: A Path into a World of Opportunities

Danabek Kaliazhdarov — Chief Executive Officer of Codiplay
Temirlan Satylkhanov — Coach of the Kazakhstan national team in competitive programming
Kuanysh Oteu — Coach of the Kazakhstan national AI team, Lead organizer of Fizmat AI Olympiad
Yeldan Abdrashim — Director of Artificial Intelligence at Allosta AI
Parasat Kyzyrkanov — Two-time ICPC finalist, ML Researcher

Annotation

The panel discussed how programming and AI olympiads become a springboard into a career: from early preparation and logical thinking to real offers and international trajectories. The Kazakhstani experience was showcased: success at the international AI olympiad was ensured by an early start and large-scale training. Age and methodological approaches, the role of English and teamwork, as well as scaling mechanisms — events, regional drivers, media, and translations of materials — were discussed.

Goals

  • Show the role of international olympiads in building a career in IT and AI
  • Define an effective training trajectory (age, foundation, training regimen)
  • Share the Kazakhstan case of organizing and winning AI olympiads
  • Discuss scaling the experience beyond elite schools to the regions
  • Identify employer requirements (English, algorithms, teamwork) and realistic expectations from olympiad achievements

Key Questions

  • From what age and how exactly should one start preparing for competitive programming and AI olympiads?
  • Which skills and foundations (logic, mathematics, data structures) are critical for success?
  • How do olympiads translate into career opportunities and job offers from companies?
  • How to scale preparation to the regions and beyond NIS/BIL/RFMSH?
  • How important are English and networking for passing interviews and working in BigTech?

Key Points

  • An early start and systematic training provide a decisive advantage in AI olympiads; in Kazakhstan, training began long before deadlines, selecting the strongest from hundreds of children.
  • Optimal path: in grades 6–7 develop logic and mathematics, from grade 8 — programming; reaching the international level requires daily practice with a coach.
  • Career foundation: algorithms and data structures, mathematical base, ability to work in a team, and system design.
  • English is a mandatory requirement: even strong olympiad participants fail interviews without confident proficiency.
  • Olympiad achievements increase the chances of job offers, but do not guarantee employment — applied preparation and additional training for industry tasks are necessary.
  • Scaling is possible through regional drivers: teacher-mentors, regular events, training camps, media coverage, and translations of materials into Kazakh/Russian.
  • The federation and the community expanded reach: open olympiads and training camps led to participation by school students from rural districts and victories on the international stage.
  • Access to knowledge is democratized: YouTube and free courses from leading universities allow one to start with minimal costs.
  • Social barriers are lower: success is determined by discipline and regularity of study, not only by “giftedness” or an elite school.
  • Digital Nomads Kazakhstan maintains the diaspora’s connection with ecosystem development inside the country, including open lectures and exchange of practices.

Quotes

“On average, it’s better to start programming from grade 8, and before that — build up logic.” — Temirlan Satylkhanov
“Our secret — we started preparing very early, while many countries got involved a month before the start.” — Kuanysh Oteu
“Without English, even finalists don’t pass interviews.” — Yeldan Abdrashim
“Children need drivers in the regions: events and coaches — then they will catch up on their own.” — Kuanysh Oteu

Conclusions

  • Programming and AI olympiads are an effective path into the industry provided there is a strong algorithmic foundation and English proficiency.
  • Early mass preparation, regional mentors, and regular events are critical for improving national results.
  • Media, translations, and open courses lower entry barriers and broaden the geography of talent beyond elite schools.

Decisions Made

  • Decided to expand the network of regional coaches and federation points of presence (Aktau, Semey, etc.) for systematic selection and training of school students.
  • Decided to translate and structure learning materials on algorithms and ML into Kazakh and Russian and distribute them to schools.
  • Decided to hold a series of open AI events and training camps (at least 3 next year) with early selection and training content.
  • Decided to launch pilot modules on ML/AI in school computer science courses with support from mentors from the community.

Action Items

  • Prepare an annual calendar of olympiads, training camps, and regional selections, including the AI track.
  • Form a list of mentors and coaches by region and launch a program for their upskilling.
  • Publish a package of open materials: roadmaps on algorithms/ML, links to courses, and a set of problems translated into Kazakh/Russian.
  • Organize a series of open lectures and hands-on workshops together with Digital Nomads Kazakhstan and partner universities.
  • Develop an English track for olympiad participants (preparation for technical interviews, speaking/tech writing) and integrate it into the training camps.
