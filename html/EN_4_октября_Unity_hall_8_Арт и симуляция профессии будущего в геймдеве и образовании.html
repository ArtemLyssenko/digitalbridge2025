Art and Simulation: Future Professions in Gamedev and Education

David Tuganov — Board Member of the Alliance of Creative Industries of Kazakhstan
Sergey (last name not specified) — gamedev expert, 19 years in the industry; CG artist/mentor, GCA Community
Aleksandra Knysheva — Head of GameLab KBTU

Annotation

The session combined an overview of creative professions in gamedev with a discussion of simulation as a new language of education. Sergey showed the full spectrum of 2D/3D artist roles and highlighted pathways into the industry without academic drawing skills. Aleksandra revealed how games model physical and social systems, influence thinking (the Super Mario and Proteus effects), and why we should develop Game Studies. Participants were invited to research and mentoring programs, as well as to take part in the Central Asia DiGRA conference.

  • Which artistic roles are in demand in 2D and 3D projects, and what skills are critical for them?
  • Do you need to know how to draw to become a CG artist, and what alternative technical roles exist?
  • How do games model reality and people (Super Mario and Proteus effects), and how can this be used in education?

Key Points

  • Gamedev projects are divided into 2D and 3D; in 2D, raster and vector graphics are important, where UI/UX, 2D animation (Spine), and FX can be done without classical drawing.
  • There are specialized roles in the 3D pipeline: concept artist, character artist, ZBrush sculptor, clothing artist (Marvelous Designer), animator/rigger, environment/vegetation artist, VFX (node-based systems), texturing (Substance Painter/Designer), level artist.
  • Some artistic tasks are solved technically: skeletal animation, procedural materials in node editors, cloth simulation — this lowers the entry barrier for those who do not "draw by hand."
  • A level designer builds playable "grey" geometry, while a level artist turns it into a final, visually rich environment.
  • The 3D generalist role is in demand but requires accumulating adjacent competencies and experience.
  • Games as a medium are unique in that they model systems that players can influence; simulations can be physical (piloting, surgery) or systemic (economy, relationships).
  • Super Mario effect: players perceive failure as a step toward success, forming a resilient learning mindset.
  • Proteus effect: identification with an avatar transfers its qualities into the player’s real behavior — this opens both educational and ethical questions.
  • Cases of influence and mirroring reality: Plague Inc., The Sims, localized worlds (Grand Mobile), representations in AAA projects and media narratives.
  • Game Lab KBTU develops learning tracks, mentorship, gamification projects with UNICEF, and conducts research on identification with a character; in February — the Central Asia DiGRA conference.

Conclusions

  • Artists can enter gamedev through a wide range of roles, including technical positions that do not require academic drawing.
  • Simulations in games are a powerful tool for learning and studying behavior; their impact requires an ethical framework and evidence-based evaluation.
  • The regional ecosystem (GCA, Game Lab KBTU, Central Asia DiGRA) provides platforms for mentorship, research, and publications, accelerating industry growth.
  • Gamification goes beyond entertainment: education, medicine, and neurotechnology already use game mechanics and interfaces.
