AI for Diplomacy: Leaders as Bridge-Builders

Laura Salamat — Director of Digital Transformation at NNEF, Women in Tech Kazakhstan
Ekaterina Smolyakova — Member of the Mazhilis of the Parliament of the Republic of Kazakhstan
Asem Bolatzhan — Advisor to the Minister of МИИЦР
Irina Sulimenko — President of «Казахстанской ассоциации информационных технологий и инноваций», CEO and founder of ТОО «КАС Консалтинг софт»
Chubak Temirov — Director of the High Technology Park of the Kyrgyz Republic

Annotation

The panel discussed AI as a new “language of diplomacy” and a trust mechanism among the state, business, and communities. Kazakhstan showcased leadership: an AI law, a national data/model platform, supercomputers, a new sectoral ministerial mandate, and a plan for an AI university. Business called for practical implementation through existing networks and reducing regulatory uncertainty, while CA IT parks urged a shift from promotion to joint technology projects (water, local languages). Communities (Women in Tech) and the financial sector proposed scalable training programs and ethical “human-in-the-loop” solutions.

Goals

  • Define the role of parliament and state institutions in supporting international cooperation and public trust during AI implementation
  • Develop practical mechanisms for cross-country technological cooperation among Central Asian IT parks
  • Increase business readiness for AI adoption through industry networks and clear, soft regulation
  • Build sustainable mass AI literacy programs (especially in regions) and community engagement
  • Align ethical principles and a human‑in‑the‑loop approach for critical domains (fintech, healthcare, public services)

Key Questions

  • What role should parliament and government bodies play in international AI cooperation and building public trust?
  • How ready is business to adopt AI, and which risks/barriers require priority removal?
  • How can we move from joint exhibitions to real inter-park technology projects between the region’s countries?
  • How can communities (e.g., Women in Tech) accelerate the export/import of knowledge and the region’s global presence?
  • Which training approaches are effective for regions with low baseline digital literacy?

Key Points

  • AI is positioned as a new tool of diplomacy: it affects negotiations, cooperation, and trust among the state, business, and society.
  • Kazakhstan is among the few countries with a separate AI law; according to Stanford — in the top 15 by the speed of regulatory adaptation.
  • Infrastructure foundations are being created: supercomputers, a national platform for datasets and training industry models, a new line agency (a minister at the rank of deputy prime minister), and a plan for an AI university.
  • The key idea of the law is balance: protect citizens’ rights without slowing technological development; soft regulation for low/medium risk, strict regulation for high risk.
  • Business insists on practicality: don’t reinvent the wheel; adapt ready-made solutions and use existing broad networks of integrators for scaling.
  • The Kyrgyz IT Park is developing an AI Academy and a community of 2000+ members, piloting a Kyrgyz-language voice assistant and proposing cross-country AI projects (water resource management).
  • Communities (Women in Tech) strengthen international presence, launch free courses (AI for Her), and are preparing a women’s venture fund to support startups.
  • The financial sector is the frontline of adoption: an AI anti-fraud case with mandatory human‑in‑the‑loop; banks help SMEs adopt AI tools.
  • Education is shifting to continuous upskilling: reskilling every 2 years; for mass regional reach — mobile‑first and gamification.
  • The trust gap and digital divide remain threats; communication, accessible training, and visible industry pilots are the main drivers of adoption.

Conclusions

  • The region needs to shift from promo activities to joint technology projects on shared infrastructure and principles of trust and ethics.
  • A combination of soft regulation, industry pilots, and integrator networks is the fastest path to scaling AI in the economy.
  • Communities and the financial sector close critical gaps: mass AI literacy, access to global markets, and practical cases with human‑in‑the‑loop.

Decisions Made

  • Initiate an official letter of recommendations from Women in Tech to the government based on the session’s outcomes.
  • Form a cross-country working group of Central Asian IT parks for joint AI pilots (priority — water resource management).
  • Scale the free AI for Her course as a regional standard of basic AI literacy (including for men).
  • Use existing industry networks of integrators (e.g., 1С) as channels for rapid AI adoption in business.
  • Adhere to a soft regulation model for low/medium risk and human‑in‑the‑loop in critical domains (fintech, healthcare).

Action Items

  • Prepare and submit to the government a package of recommendations: inter-park cooperation, dataset sharing, ethical frameworks, and training programs.
  • Launch a pilot cross-country AI project on water resources (Kazakhstan–Kyrgyzstan–Uzbekistan–Tajikistan) with shared metrics and data.
  • Create a catalog of existing integrator and implementer networks by industry and agree on AI implementation roadmaps with them.
  • Deploy a regional online AI learning platform (AI for Her) with mobile access and gamification to reach the regions.
  • Organize series of industry trust pilots (anti-fraud, healthcare diagnostics, EdTech) with public reporting on metrics and risks.
  • Form a communications campaign on AI ethics and protection of citizens’ rights, including explanation of regulation and human‑in‑the‑loop mechanics.
