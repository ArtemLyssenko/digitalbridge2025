The impact of artificial intelligence on the future of space initiatives

Mark Lemin — SpaceTech and innovation expert, France
Amanat Umbetbayev — Managing Director – Member of the Management Board, JSC NC Kazakhstan Gharysh Sapary
Vasiliy Leonov — Republican Center for Space Communications of Kazakhstan
Darius Kuliesius — Communications regulator, Lithuania

Annotation

The panel discussed how AI is already applied across the entire space stack — from telemetry processing and EO on Earth to onboard autonomy and data filtering on spacecraft. Key drivers highlighted: the explosion of data volumes, the need for mission autonomy, cybersecurity, spectrum management, and the upcoming 6G. Methods for ensuring reliability were considered (phased deployment, confidence thresholds, human-in-the-loop) as well as regulatory tools (sandboxes, the risk-based EU AI Act). Challenges identified: radiation tolerance of chips, limited onboard compute, lack of training data, and a real escalation of cyberthreats.

Goals

  • Provide an overview of current and prospective AI applications in the space sector (on the ground and onboard).
  • Identify key drivers and use cases: big data, autonomy, cybersecurity, spectrum management, and 6G.
  • Discuss reliability risks and accountability: AI hallucinations, human-in-the-loop, standards, and legal frameworks.
  • Formulate an approach to cyber-protecting space infrastructure using AI and sharing best practices.
  • Synchronize the vision on regulatory tools (sandboxes, risk-based regulation) for Central Asian countries.

Key Questions

  • Where and how widely is AI used in space today — on the ground and onboard satellites/robots?
  • Which drivers make AI indispensable in the space sector (data volumes, autonomy, cybersecurity, spectrum, 6G)?
  • How to ensure the reliability of AI outputs, and who should make final decisions in critical scenarios?
  • What are the key cyberthreats to space infrastructure, and how does AI help detect and prevent them?
  • What are the technological, ethical, and regulatory challenges (radiation, data, accountability), and which tools address them (regulatory sandboxes, EU AI Act)?

Key Points

  • The volumes of telemetry and EO imagery (tens of billions of rows, terabytes of images per day) require AI for analysis, anomaly detection, and decision support.
  • AI is already used onboard small satellites: cloud filtering, data compression, preliminary selection of useful frames, and elements of autonomous maneuvering.
  • Autonomy: a shift from 1–2 days of automated operation to weeks and the potential for months thanks to AI amid limited communication links.
  • Cybersecurity is a critical priority: protection of TT&C radio links, anomaly detection, prevention of interception/jamming/spoofing.
  • AI-driven spectrum management helps promptly identify sources of interference and increase frequency-use efficiency.
  • 6G and AI-enabled integration of space and terrestrial networks can expand connectivity for 2.7 billion unconnected users.
  • AI verification: phased rollout, confidence thresholds, human-in-the-loop, and standards from sectoral agencies improve detection accuracy.
  • Regulation: the risk-based EU AI Act, distributed responsibility (manufacturer, operator, regulator), and regulatory sandboxes as a tool for safe testing.
  • Technical barriers: radiation tolerance of AI chips, energy-efficient onboard computing, and the need for synthetic data for training.
  • Ethical aspects and dual-use: autonomous decisions must align with human objectives, safety standards, and transparency.

Conclusions

  • AI is becoming an integral layer of the space ecosystem — from ground analytics to onboard autonomy — but it requires rigorous verification practices and human-centered control.
  • Cyber resilience and AI-enabled spectrum management are priority areas for protecting critical space services.
  • Regulatory sandboxes and risk-based frameworks (EU legislation) are a practical path to responsible AI integration in space with clear distributed responsibility.

Decisions Made

  • Initiate the creation of a regulatory sandbox for AI in space services in Central Asia with methodological support from the Lithuanian regulator.
  • Support the development and piloting of onboard AI functions (cloud filtering, compression, autonomous maneuvering) on upcoming Kazakh missions.
  • Strengthen the cybersecurity of space infrastructure by deploying ML-based anomaly detection systems and protection of TT&C radio links.
  • Align the future Digital Code of Kazakhstan with risk-based AI regulation principles and EU practices.

Action Items

  • Audit sources of telemetry and EO and deploy synthetic data pipelines for model training.
  • Create a matrix of AI confidence thresholds and escalation rules to the operator (human-in-the-loop) for critical scenarios.
  • Organize a joint workshop of KGS/РЦКК and the Lithuanian regulator on regulatory sandboxes and the application of the EU AI Act.
  • Prepare an onboard satellite PoC for cloud detection and data compression with an assessment of traffic savings and quality.
  • Deploy an AI-based spectrum monitoring system to detect interference/spoofing and rapidly localize sources.
  • Define requirements for radiation-hardened computing modules for AI and plan in-orbit tests.
