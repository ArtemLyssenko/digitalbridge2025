AI: The Future of Learning

Annotation

The panel discussed how AI is changing education: from personalization and shifting the teacher’s role to mentorship to integrating AI into real school processes. Conditions for success were emphasized: combining technology with pedagogical methodology, localization for the Kazakh language and curricula, as well as optimizing cost and infrastructure (including offline modes). Experts called for cautious, metrics-driven scaling through pilots, prioritizing teacher retraining and reducing inequality.

Goals

  • Form a shared vision for applying AI in education and the teacher’s role in the new model
  • Define priority pilot areas and metrics of their effectiveness
  • Understand risks: inequality, cognitive debt, excesses of 'overhype,' and cost of ownership
  • Outline technical approaches to localization (Kazakh language) and cost reduction of solutions
  • Align steps to prepare teachers and administrators to work with AI

Key Questions

  • How will the teacher’s role change with personalized pathways and adaptive learning?
  • Which real AI cases in schools produce measurable impact, and how should it be measured?
  • Is AI capable of increasing productivity and employment, or will it displace jobs?
  • How to ensure AI scaling in the education system considering language, cost, and infrastructure (including offline)?
  • How to reduce educational inequality and avoid 'overhype' when implementing AI?

Key Points

  • AI unlocks personalization and adaptive pathways: from a 'class-centric' to a 'learner-centric' model with epistemic maps for each student.
  • The teacher’s role shifts from a knowledge holder to a mentor, tutor, motivator, and facilitator of team work.
  • EdTech success with AI requires aligning technology with domain expertise and methodology; 'PDF-ization' is not digitalization.
  • Simple wins are already available: teacher assistants for routine tasks, content generation, grading, recommendations.
  • The 'virtual classmate' model (an AI partner) enhances learning through explanation and teaching.
  • Key reasons for implementation failures: lack of customization to curriculum/language, ignoring adaptive monitoring for teachers/the system, and unaccounted costs (GPU, scale).
  • Technical paths to cost reduction: LLM distillation and quantization, ensembles of specialized small models, orchestration, semantic caching, federated learning, offline and on-device modes.
  • Kazakhstan has a consolidation advantage (unified standards/data) and a window of opportunity for localization to the Kazakh language and rapid pilots.
  • Risks: growth of cognitive debt, increased inequality (urban–rural, language of instruction), overhype, market fragmentation, and dependence on a single vendor.
  • Objective metrics, cautious scaling through pilots, training of teachers and a community of practice, as well as 'AI-free zones' are needed to foster socialization and reading.

Conclusions

  • AI already delivers practical value in teachers’ routine tasks and in personalization, but scaling requires localization to the language/curriculum and rigorous effectiveness evaluation.
  • Priority goes to training teachers and administrators, creating metrics and safe infrastructure (including offline modes) to reduce inequality and cognitive risks.
  • Kazakhstan can accelerate through centralized pilots, a registry of vetted solutions, and a program to translate/create high-quality content in the Kazakh language.
  • A technically resilient path is small and specialized models with orchestration, caching, and federated learning to reduce TCO and dependence on clouds.

Decisions Made

  • Publish and present a foresight report on AI in education and use it as the basis for pilots and metrics.
  • Expand pilots of AI solutions at 'Келешек мектептері' sites with a focus on teacher assistants and adaptive monitoring.
  • Launch a mass retraining program for teachers and principals on working with AI, cyber hygiene, and ethics.
  • Begin forming a state registry of approved EdTech solutions and verification/safety criteria.
  • Create a localization initiative for AI in the Kazakh language: translation and adaptation of high-quality educational content.
  • Enshrine the principle of cautious scaling: all implementations go through pilots with objective metrics and feedback.

Action Items

  • Define a set of target metrics (achievement, progress along pathways, teacher time savings, engagement, impact on the urban–rural gap).
  • Form an interagency working group (Ministry of Education, IT companies, educators) for AI customization and data standardization.
  • Launch a translation/localization program for the Kazakh language with the involvement of teachers and high school students; create open data corpora.
  • Conduct an open RFP/market scan of EdTech with a series of controlled pilots and public evaluation of results.
  • Deploy teacher AI assistants for routine tasks (grading, feedback, planning) in pilot schools.
  • Develop an architecture for low-cost deployments: small models, orchestration, semantic caching, on-device/offline modes, and federated learning.
  • Create leadership schools for principals and learning circles for innovative teachers to share cases and upskill.
  • Prepare regulatory requirements for privacy, security, AI ethics, and accessibility for schools.
  • Test the balance of 'AI-free zones' and digital practices to foster socialization, reading, and teamwork.
  • Ensure transparent reporting on pilots (dashboard with metrics and external review) and a plan to scale successful solutions.
