Japan and Korea: how to build startup ecosystems and bridges with Central Asia

Shugyla Sagynbekova — Startup Consultant, TechWomen’24
Dzheyon Pak — CEO of The Garrison and organizer of ForsterBridge
Dana MakKo — Head of IVS Youth
Viplus Wang — Head of IVS Global, Headline VC

Annotation

The session showed how Japan and Korea build ecosystems through universities, government, and business, and where Central Asia fits in. Opportunities of IVS (including IVS Youth and Launchpad) and the global fund Headline VC were presented. The engineer shortage in Japan and employment channels via People Cloud were discussed. A separate emphasis was placed on inclusive programs for women and people with disabilities, as well as potential formats for international cooperation with the countries of Central Asia.

  • Show youth and entrepreneurial opportunities of IVS and their scaling beyond Japan
  • Form an understanding of priority sectors for venture investments and startup expansion into global markets
  • Connect engineering talent from Central Asia with Japanese demand via People Cloud and adjacent channels

Key Points

  • IVS started in 2007 in Miyazaki as an invite-only boutique conference; since 2023 it has been open to all, 13,000 participants in the main part and about 500 side events over 3–4 days, totaling up to 50,000 participants in the host city (Kyoto).
  • IVS’s flagship program is Launchpad: over 18 years, hundreds of finalists, around 30 IPOs and ~35 M&A; most Japanese unicorns presented at IVS at early stages.
  • Headline VC is a global fund (~$4B AUM) with offices in America, Europe, Latin America, and Asia; focus on global founders and markets.
  • Priority investment areas: e-commerce, web/internet services, AI, entertainment, commercial technologies; strong categories of Japanese unicorns — B2B internet services (including HR/Smart), B2C apps (e.g., Mercari), deeptech/materials.
  • VC market comparison: Japan ~$20B, Korea ~$8.8B, Kazakhstan ~$71M; VC as a share of GDP in Japan is low but growing; Central Asia has the potential for rapid growth with access to international capital.
  • People Cloud helps engineers from Eastern Europe and Central Asia get jobs in Japan: a shortage of hundreds of thousands of engineers, a visa is now available if you have an offer; the language barrier is easing, and the number of positions for non-Japanese speakers is growing.
  • Practical employment channels: the 'Hello, Япония' project, the Tokyo Dev and LinkedIn platforms; People Cloud runs free cohorts for Japanese-language courses and familiarization visits to Japan.
  • Japan is accelerating entrepreneurship education: university entrepreneurship departments, project-based learning in schools, student and school pitch competitions (including IVS Youth).
  • Korea actively subsidizes company launches with dedicated tracks (for women, foreigners, 60+), including monthly grants for 6 months when registering a business in the country.
  • Cultural shift in Japan: from lifetime employment to greater mobility; startups need to balance traditional quality with the need for speed in hypothesis testing.

Conclusions

  • The university–business–government linkage with an emphasis on youth and inclusion fosters sustainable ecosystem growth (as seen in IVS and Korean programs).
  • Central Asia is on the verge of scaling: plugging into IVS platforms and global capital (Headline VC and others) can speed up the emergence of global projects and talent mobility.
