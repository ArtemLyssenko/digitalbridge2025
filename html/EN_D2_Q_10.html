AI in Education

Piter Norvig — Google and Stanford expert

Annotation

Piter Norvig showed how AI is moving from massive online courses to personal tutors and assistants for teachers, grounded in the principles of active and mastery learning. It was discussed how to make AI agents pedagogical (not just giving answers, but guiding), and why specialized data and student simulations are needed. The issues of LLM hallucinations and motivation were considered, as well as the prospect of lifelong learning.

Goals

  • Make a personal AI tutor available to every learner.
  • Equip every teacher with an AI assistant for preparing and adapting materials.
  • Embed principles of active and mastery learning into AI tools.
  • Create realistic student simulations based on specialized data to accelerate experiments in education
  • Reduce hallucinations and increase trust in AI for learning and search

Key Questions

  • How to combine AI personalization with sound pedagogy so that the agent guides rather than gives the answer?
  • What data and methods are needed for plausible simulations of student behavior that go beyond the internet?
  • How to balance LLM creativity and factuality, reducing hallucinations in educational scenarios?
  • Are different AI approaches needed for STEM and the humanities, where there is no 'right answer'?
  • Why are motivation and completion falling in online courses, and can AI raise them?

Key Points

  • Historical context: from Socratic dialogue to mass classrooms — AI opens a chance to return to individual mentoring.
  • Experiments with MOOC (2011) yielded only ~10% improvement; modern models allow building full-fledged pedagogical agents.
  • A pedagogical agent does not give a ready-made answer, but leads through the steps of a solution (example: Khanmigo at Khan Academy).
  • Mastery before creativity: a shift to a 'complete the topic to mastery' model, with adaptation of pace and workload.
  • LearnLM (Google) is aimed at active learning, managing cognitive load, diagnosing progress, and stimulating curiosity.
  • Automation of authoring work: from a textbook, AI generates multimodal materials (video/podcast/visualizations) tailored to the student profile.
  • Fast iteration requires student simulations; internet corpora are insufficient — domain and behavioral data are required.
  • Lessons from autonomous cars: first real data collection, then scale via simulations — an analogous path in EdTech.
  • Reducing hallucinations: task design, learning with feedback, and a 'penalty for guessing' encourage abstaining from answering when uncertain.
  • Education is broader than knowledge transfer: judgment, motivation, social responsibility, and lifelong learning matter.

Quotes

“We want not a chatbot with answers, but an educator who guides and helps you do the work yourself.” — Piter Norvig

Conclusions

  • AI is ready to support mastery-based and personalized learning, strengthening rather than replacing the teacher's role.
  • Plausible student simulations and specialized datasets will be the key to a step-change acceleration of innovation in education.
  • Controlling hallucinations and sound task design are critically important for trust in AI in learning scenarios.

Decisions Made

  • Orient the design of educational AI agents toward pedagogical guidance rather than simple answer delivery.
  • Focus tool development on helping authors and teachers adapt content to individual trajectories.
  • Collect and curate specialized educational and behavioral data (with consent), supplementing internet corpora.
  • Apply strategies of penalizing unjustified guesses and learning with feedback to reduce LLM hallucinations.

Action Items

  • Launch pilots with LearnLM/analogs in real classrooms with metrics of mastery and engagement.
  • Develop a pipeline for generating multimodal learning materials from textbooks with control of pedagogical goals.
  • Create protocols for collecting specialized data and ethical consent procedures (including optional biosignals).
  • Build and validate student simulations based on LLM agents with various personae and learning styles.
  • Implement in AI agents mechanisms for confidence calibration and 'refusal to answer' at low confidence.
  • Define success metrics beyond knowledge tests: progress toward mastery, independence, motivation, and transfer of skills.
