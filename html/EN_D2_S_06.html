Hardware backbone of innovation: from chips to robots in the era of the generative nation

Annotation

The panel discussed how hardware infrastructure (chips, data centers, logistics) is becoming the foundation for deploying AI and robotics. Robot manufacturers highlighted the main scaling obstacles: cost, shortages of data and models, as well as manipulators/grippers. Kazakhstan was considered a promising node of digital and physical infrastructure with pilots in drone delivery and warehouse automation. Companies are targeting hybrid models: combining in-house hardware, open SDKs, and co-developing use cases with customers.

Goals

  • Define the role of hardware infrastructure as the foundation for the growth of AI and robotics
  • Formulate priority use cases and monetization models (including Robots-as-a-Service)
  • Assess scaling challenges: cost, data/models, skills, and regulation
  • Outline partnerships and pilots in Kazakhstan for logistics, warehouses, and digital hubs
  • Agree on an approach to data collection and the creation of agent and digital twin platforms

Key Questions

  • How to accelerate the deployment of compute and logistics infrastructure for AI?
  • Which robotics use cases are most economically justified in the next 3–5 years?
  • Which business model is more effective: direct sales or end-to-end Robots-as-a-Service?
  • How to solve the shortage of data and models for autonomous robots and manipulators?
  • What role can Kazakhstan play as a regional hub and testbed for pilots?

Key Points

  • Globally there are more than 11,000 data centers and over 8 million AI servers shipped; by 2030 the world will require large-scale investment in compute capacity.
  • Lenovo is pursuing a hybrid strategy: AI‑accelerated manufacturing, smart warehouses, and digital twins; more than 150 million units of computing solutions have been delivered to customers.
  • Kazakhstan is viewed by Lenovo as a neighboring market with logistical advantages (BRI/Belt and Road Initiative) and potential for creating digital and physical hubs.
  • The robotics market is growing: according to IFR, more than 440 manufacturers and ~200 thousand sales; top scenarios — logistics, service, cleaning, agriculture, safety/rescue.
  • Unitree: the main barriers of the next decade are the lack of data and efficient models for robots; manipulation tasks are especially challenging (grippers ≠ human fingers).
  • Open SDKs/APIs and a DIY approach are important for customization to industry scenarios and for accelerating secondary development on the customer side.
  • Deep Robotics started with industrial tasks (inspection of electrical substations), the focus is to create real value in applied scenarios and bring robots into everyday life.
  • Freedom Holding is launching drone‑delivery pilots in Almaty, plans to automate 10–20 warehouses, and to run AI models locally on NVIDIA GPUs to orchestrate the robot fleet.
  • The key operational challenge is management scalability: today one operator manages one robot; the goal is 1 operator for 4–5 robots.
  • Reducing cost through scale and standardization is critical for mass adoption of robots in everyday life and service industries.

Conclusions

  • The hardware backbone is the key to scaling AI: without compute, logistics, and energy foundations, the application of AI and robots is limited.
  • The next mass use cases for robots are logistics and service; household scenarios will accelerate as hardware gets cheaper and data/models emerge.
  • Kazakhstan is ready to play the role of a regional testbed for pilots and a hub, provided business, regulators, and universities cooperate.

Decisions Made

  • Lenovo will work out partnerships in Kazakhstan to develop digital and warehouse infrastructure and to create a regional hub.
  • Unitree and Deep Robotics will maintain open SDKs/APIs and expand joint pilots with local customers for industry customization.
  • Freedom Holding will launch phased pilots: last‑mile drone delivery and warehouse automation with assessment of economic impact.
  • The parties agree to coordinate data collection and labeling to train robotics and digital twin models.
  • Focus on reducing the TCO of robots through scaling and standardization of components/software.

Action Items

  • Form a working group (Lenovo, local operators, regulators) to design a digital/physical hub and smart warehouses in the Republic of Kazakhstan.
  • Launch 2–3 industry RaaS pilots: infrastructure inspection, in‑plant logistics, security/patrol.
  • Deploy a drone‑delivery pilot in Almaty with SLA, safety, and economic metrics; prepare to scale to 5+ locations.
  • Build a data pipeline for robots: collection, simulation, labeling; launch training of small models/agents on local GPUs.
  • Conduct a techno‑economic assessment of automating 10–20 warehouses: ROI, CAPEX/OPEX, WMS/OMS integration.
  • Prepare regulatory sandboxes for ground robots and cooperative control (1 operator per 4–5 devices).
