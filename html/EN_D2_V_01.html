Keynote speech: The real reason AI fails in business: implementation, not models

Dilshat Uteshev — Executive Director for AI at JPMorgan Chase, advisor to the AI company shai.pro

Annotation

The speaker showed that AI pilots fail more often because behavior and processes remain unchanged, not because of model quality. A frame was proposed: design for three user archetypes (skeptics, implementers, champions) and sell not AI, but a measurable outcome. Trust is ensured by control mechanisms and 'red zones', and adoption happens through habit formation. The maturity path: Demo → Habit → System → Culture, with native AI integration into work tools.

Goals

  • Identify systemic reasons for low AI adoption after successful demos.
  • Define user roles and the conditions required for AI adoption (mandate, support, metrics).
  • Form trust and control practices: red zones, manual review, reversibility.
  • Show a roadmap for moving from POC to a culture of AI use with measurable KPIs.

Key Questions

  • Why do AI pilots not survive after a successful demo?
  • Who is the real buyer and process owner, and which KPIs matter to them?
  • What safeguards and 'red zones' are needed to build trust in AI and manage errors?
  • How do you lead a team through the stages Demo → Habit → System → Culture, and when should you scale?
  • When should AI 'dissolve' into the process, and when should it change the team's way of working itself?

Key Points

  • The main reason for failures is the lack of changes in behavior and processes, not the models' technical limitations.
  • There are three user archetypes: skeptics, implementers, and champions; each needs its own motivators, support, and mandate.
  • You should sell not AI, but an improved outcome under the same constraints; you must clearly define the buyer, process owner, and KPIs.
  • Political risks and accountability slow adoption: AI removes the 'safety net', increasing fear of mistakes.
  • Trust is built on control, not just accuracy: red zones, manual review, 'undo/explain' buttons, escalation protocols.
  • Habits matter more than training: reinforce AI use through daily practices and champions' success stories.
  • Maturity ladder: from demo to habit, then to system and only then to culture; scaling is possible after planning the next step.
  • Native AI integration into work tools (e.g., Cursor) reduces friction and makes AI a 'second pilot'.

Conclusions

  • Organizational changes, clear roles, and metrics are mandatory conditions for moving from demo to real AI use.
  • Without safeguards and a habit culture, even strong POCs don't scale.
  • Native AI integration into processes reduces friction and fear, accelerating adoption.

Decisions Made

  • Define and regulate 'red zones' and control procedures for AI use.
  • Appoint champions in key teams and give them a mandate to implement, with mandatory collection of success stories.
  • Approve a roadmap for the transition Demo → Habit → System → Culture with KPIs and stage gates at each phase.

Action Items

  • Inventory target processes: assign owners, identify buyers, and define KPIs for tasks where AI will deliver measurable value.
  • Launch limited pilots on routine tasks with champions and weekly tracking of impact (time, quality, cost).
  • Deploy trust mechanisms: 'undo/explain' buttons, an activity log, manual review for the 'yellow zone'.
  • Set up regular team rituals (dailies, demos, retros) to cement habits and share AI use cases.
  • Integrate AI natively into the tools in use (IDE, CRM, ticketing), eliminating copy-paste between windows.
