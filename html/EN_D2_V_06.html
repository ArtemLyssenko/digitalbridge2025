Keynote speech: Digital inclusion in a world oriented toward artificial intelligence

Акинсанми Тити — Thought leader in digital economy public policy, Global Digital Inclusion Partnership, Member of the Advisory Board of the Tech Diplomacy Forum

Annotation

The talk emphasized that digital inclusion is more than handing out devices: it is access, voice, and agency in the digital environment. Data were presented on 2.6 billion offline users and the urban–rural divide (83% versus 48%). Emphasis was placed on the need for proactive, locally relevant policy and the inclusion of civil society. A call to move from mere access to meaningful connectivity and citizens’ digital agency by 2030.

Goals

  • Rethink digital inclusion as a combination of access, rights, participation, and agency.
  • Reduce the urban–rural divide in connectivity and quality of access.
  • Achieve meaningful connectivity for the global majority by 2030.
  • Create proactive, localized, and future-oriented digital policies and regulations.
  • Strengthen citizens’ digital and political literacy and the engagement of civil society.

Key Questions

  • How can we ensure “meaningful connectivity” (frequency, 4G speeds, device, access at home/at work/in education) for billions of people by 2030?
  • How to balance the speed of innovation (“rabbit”) and regulation (“tortoise”) without stifling innovation?
  • What mechanisms ensure that all stakeholders are represented at the table, including rural communities and vulnerable groups?
  • How to avoid copy-pasting other countries’ policies and achieve local relevance and effectiveness?
  • What digital and political literacy tools do users need for informed consent and data protection?

Key Points

  • 2.6 billion people remain offline out of a global population of about 8 billion; the gap is especially pronounced in rural areas.
  • Digital inclusion is not only devices and connectivity, but also a voice, participation in rule-making, and a sense of agency.
  • Urban–rural divide in Internet use: 83% versus 48%, requiring targeted policy and investment.
  • The shift to “meaningful connectivity” includes daily internet access, speeds no less than 4G, a smartphone, and access in key settings of daily life.
  • Regulations are often reactive and lag behind innovation; a proactive, future-oriented regulatory framework is needed.
  • Direct copy-pasting of policies from other countries is unacceptable; localization and contextualization are necessary.
  • Digital and political literacy are critical: users must understand agreements, risk disclosures, and data processing rules.
  • Civil society is not optional but a mandatory participant in developing digital policy and advocating for the interests of the majority.
  • Tech diplomacy and appointing tech diplomats can level the power imbalance between states and major technology players.

Conclusions

  • Without proactive, locally adapted policy, the digital divide will widen, exacerbating the exclusion of groups and the power imbalance.
  • The shift from “access” to “agency” is key to sustainable digital inclusion and human development.

Decisions Made

  • Support the goal of achieving meaningful connectivity for the global majority by 2030.
  • Initiate the development of localized, future-oriented digital policies instead of copying others’ models.
  • Include civil society and representatives of rural communities in the process of creating regulations and digital literacy programs.

Action Items

  • Conduct an audit of the urban–rural gap in connectivity and quality of access, disaggregated by regions and vulnerable groups.
  • Launch digital and political literacy programs with an emphasis on informed consent, safety, and data governance.
  • Create a multistakeholder working group (government, business, NGOs, academia) for proactive AI/tech regulation.
  • Design and pilot localization of digital public infrastructure modeled on successful examples (without direct copy-paste).
  • Prepare a tech diplomacy initiative, including appointing a tech diplomat and standards for engagement with large platforms.
  • Implement plain-language versions of user agreements and risk notification mechanisms for mass-market services.
