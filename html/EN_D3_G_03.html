The GPT Generation: How to Live and Think in the Age of AI

Annotation

The discussion showed that AI has already reshaped media, healthcare, and everyday life: from phygital clinics and wearables to generative content and new creative professions. Speakers emphasized the need for balance between algorithmic efficiency and human leadership — critical thinking, empathy, and responsibility. Risks (regulation, fakes, dependency, AGI) and digital hygiene practices for children were discussed. Media and creators see GPT as an accelerator, not a replacement, while the value of “live” human creativity is growing.

Goals

  • Define the role of humans and media in the age of AI and preserve the leadership of human judgment
  • Show practical opportunities for integrating AI into content, business, and medicine
  • Formulate approaches to critical thinking, fact-checking, and digital hygiene (especially for children)
  • Outline ethical boundaries and the need for regulation (AI “red lines”)
  • Encourage the participation of young people and women in creating algorithms and AI startups

Key Questions

  • How do we preserve human leadership in a world of algorithms and avoid handing key decisions over to AI?
  • Can the GPT generation self-govern through technology, or does mindfulness remain decisive?
  • What role should media play in the age of AI, and what should they steer when algorithms shape the agenda?
  • Is GPT a threat to industries or a new era of growth opportunities?
  • How can we foster focus and critical thinking in children amid information noise and easily accessible AI tools?

Key Points

  • AI needs “red lines”: humans must retain control and practice fact-checking and critical thinking in decision-making.
  • Healthcare is rapidly becoming phygital: collecting personal data from wearables and home sensors will speed up diagnostics and personalize therapy.
  • Media are already integrating generative tools (example: Hixfield) to accelerate content production and visualizations, but the creative idea remains with humans.
  • The entertainment industry is migrating from TV to digital platforms; GPT accelerates processes, reduces routine, and makes teams more productive.
  • AI risks include fakes/disinformation, deepfake voices/videos, dependency, and the need for interstate regulation (analogous to nuclear energy).
  • The value of “live” experience and craft will grow: music, cooking, art, and offline interactions are becoming premium.
  • For children, digital hygiene (notification settings, “no-phones” rules), gamified learning, and the practice of open-ended questions are critical.
  • Generation Z/Alpha perceives AI as a natural tool on which startups and educational solutions are built.
  • Women’s participation in algorithm development is necessary to minimize bias and broaden perspectives in AI.

Conclusions

  • AI is an accelerator, not a replacement: human leadership is based on critical thinking, empathy, and responsibility.
  • Media, business, and healthcare benefit from AI integration, but require ethical boundaries, fact-checking, and sound regulation.
  • Digital hygiene and focus skills in children are key to safe and productive coexistence with technology.

Decisions Made

  • Support the principle of AI “red lines” and the need for cross-industry and government regulation.
  • Media will continue integrating AI tools (video and text generation) with an emphasis on fact-checking and ethics.
  • Launch/scale a phygital approach in clinics using wearables data for personalized medicine.
  • Develop and promote educational content on critical thinking and digital hygiene for schools and families.
  • Promote the participation of women and youth in developing algorithms and creating AI startups (mentorship, awards, hackathons).

Action Items

  • Form a working group on AI ethics and regulation (media, IT companies, doctors, lawyers) and prepare recommendations.
  • Implement newsroom standards for fact-checking content created with AI, and AI-generated labels.
  • Launch a pilot in clinics: collect sleep/glucose/activity data (Whoop, Garmin, Oura, glucose monitors) and integrate into treatment plans.
  • Create a series of short TikTok/YouTube Shorts videos on digital hygiene: “dinner without phones,” how to turn off notifications, how to spot fakes.
  • Hold workshops for Gen Z/Alpha creators on prompt engineering, AI ethics, and the legal risks of deepfakes.
  • Launch a mentorship program for girls in AI/data together with media and IT companies.
