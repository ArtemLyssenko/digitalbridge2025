Drones and Artificial Intelligence: from Security to Logistics and Infrastructure

Dmitry Ivanov — Director of Aardvark
Darkhan Akhmediev — Deputy Minister of Defense of the Republic of Kazakhstan
Yerlan Yesimseitov — Executive Director of Freedom Lifestyle
Ablaihan Ospanov — Managing Director for Digitalization at Samruk-Kazyna

Annotation

The panel showed how the combination of drones and AI is already changing security, logistics, and infrastructure: the Ministry of Defense of the Republic of Kazakhstan has deployed operator training and is streamlining NLAs; Freedom Lifestyle has launched R&D and the first deliveries by cargo drones; Kazakhtelecom uses a fleet of UAVs for road control, flood response, and ecology; all participants agreed on the need for a national UTM system and updated rules for mass flights in cities.

Goals

  • Discuss the shift from pilots to scaling the use of AI-enabled drones in defense, logistics, and urban management.
  • Align regulation (registration, licensing, flight zones) and define requirements for the national UTM system.
  • Expand training of UAV operators and technical specialists through «Сарбас плюс» and private schools.
  • Establish industry–government cooperation via Defence Tech for pilots and deployments.
  • Assess the prerequisites for local production/assembly of drones given consolidated demand.

Key Questions

  • How is the Ministry of Defense of the Republic of Kazakhstan structuring UAV development and AI integration, including operator training and NLAs?
  • What mechanisms are needed to legalize and scale the number of operators in the civil sector?
  • Is drone delivery hype or an operationally and economically realistic service?
  • What regulatory barriers in Astana and Almaty impede mass flights, and how can they be removed?
  • How to ensure safety with hundreds of simultaneous flights, and what role will a national UTM system play?

Key Points

  • A UAV development department has been established within the Ministry of Defense of the Republic of Kazakhstan; civil regulation is handled by the Ministry of Transport/Civil Aviation Committee (CAC), and military regulation by the MoD (especially in wartime).
  • The «Сарбас плюс» program has been launched: training UAV operators, communications specialists, and technical personnel based at an Air Defense unit in Almaty; applications via MyArmy.kz, upgraded infrastructure, and employment opportunities.
  • Defence Tech acts as a “bridge” between the security/defense sector and startups: incubation, acceleration, and piloting of solutions; expanding presence in Astana Hub and launching an office in Almaty.
  • Freedom Lifestyle is conducting R&D on cargo drones, has completed theory for 3rd-category operators, and is launching practice and licensing jointly with the Aviation Administration of Kazakhstan (AAK); the goal is regular deliveries within a 3–4 km radius from warehouses by the end of the year.
  • Delivery economics are still in R&D: technologies are expected to become cheaper over a 2–3 year horizon; the key bottleneck is a dense network of dark stores and standardized 10×10 m pads.
  • Platforms used: DJI and ARC; customization is required (winch, rain resistance) for local conditions and scenarios.
  • Kazakhtelecom operates ~60 drones for road enforcement (recording illegal overtakes), flood monitoring, forest fires, and detecting nighttime dumps in Astana; AI helps build 3D models and track dynamics.
  • Mass flights in megacities are impossible without updating rules, simplifying permits, and launching an independent national low-altitude UTM system; the industry is ready to develop a UTM and hand it over to the state.
  • Demand consolidation in national companies can justify local assembly/production, but there are questions of cost and safety requirements.

Conclusions

  • The combination of drones and AI already delivers measurable benefits in security, ecology, and logistics; scaling is constrained by talent and rules.
  • Mass urban flights require updated NLAs, simplified permitting procedures, and a national UTM system.
  • Industrial cooperation via Defence Tech and the participation of national companies accelerate adoption and reduce risks.

Decisions Made

  • Launch joint work with the AAK and the Ministry of Transport to shape requirements for the national UTM system and simplify permitting procedures in megacities.
  • Open the Defence Tech platform to receive AI/UAV startups and organize pilots in the security/defense sector.
  • Freedom Lifestyle continues obtaining 3rd-category operator status and begins regular deliveries within a 3–4 km radius from warehouses by the end of the year.
  • Samruk-Kazyna JSC consolidates intra-holding demand for UAVs to assess the feasibility of local assembly.

Action Items

  • Form an industry working group (MoD, Ministry of Transport/CAC/AAK, Samruk, Freedom, Kazakhtelecom, associations) on VLL and UTM rules.
  • Prepare a technical UTM roadmap: architecture, identification, corridors and buffers, integration with the CAC, reliability requirements.
  • Scale training: expand intake into «Сарбас плюс», accredit private schools, develop unified standards for 3rd-category operators.
  • Run urban BVLOS/cargo flight pilots in Almaty and on the periphery of Astana with safe routes and risk measurements.
  • Standardize pad infrastructure at warehouses (10×10 m), safety regulations, and pre-flight procedures.
  • Assemble a pool of needs from national companies and telecom operators to calculate TCO and the techno-economic justification of local assembly.
  • Prepare proposals for API submission and SLA processing of flight applications for mass delivery services.
