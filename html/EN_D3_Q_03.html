Robots Without Borders: Global Innovations Meet Kazakhstan's Ambitions

Asylbek Murzakhmetov — Head of the Public Foundation 'USTEM Foundation'
Bayan Konirbayev — Deputy Chair of the Board of Trustees of alem.ai
Chi Chu — Founder of Cosmicbrain.ai
Zhanat Kappasov — Doctor of Science in Robotics
Azamat Esmukhametov — Doctor of Technical Sciences and Candidate of Technical Sciences

Annotation

The panel discussed the shift from AI in software to 'physical AI' in robots, priorities for Kazakhstan (mining industry, energy, agriculture, and smart cities), as well as regulation and data sovereignty. Technology gaps in mechanics/actuators and the critical role of tactile sensors were noted. Attention was given to workforce development, cultural acceptance of robots, and linking universities with industry.

Goals

  • Identify applied niches for robotics in Kazakhstan with maximum impact on safety and productivity
  • Form an approach to regulating AI and robotics that balances innovation and data protection
  • Bridge educational achievements (competition robotics) with industry needs and pilots
  • Identify technology priorities in the hardware of robots (power systems, actuators, tactile sensors)

Key Questions

  • What regulatory balance should Kazakhstan choose between European strictness and American flexibility?
  • Which sectors to robotize as a priority (mining, energy, agriculture, infrastructure inspections)?
  • Which technology gaps hinder mass deployment of robots (actuators, mechanics, sensors, energy efficiency)?
  • How to ensure sovereignty and security of robot data amid cloud computing outside the country?

Key Points

  • The distinction between AI in software and 'physical AI' (robots) drives investment strategy: software is overheated; hardware is underfunded.
  • Humanoid form increases trust and acceptance of robots by people, but not all tasks require humanoids.
  • Simulations and generative AI allow training robots in skills without costly physical testbeds.
  • For smart cities, there is demand for autonomous delivery, mapping, sensor networks, and reducing bias in data collection.
  • Tactile feedback is critical: without touch, a robot will not achieve the speed and reliability of human manipulation.
  • Inspection robots for pipelines and infrastructure are a fast path to improved reliability and safety.
  • Data and cloud: local infrastructure, localization, and constraints on cross-border transfer of robot data are necessary.
  • Cultural integration and education (mathematics, physics) build long-term trust and competencies in robotics.
  • Robotization carries social risks of worker displacement; proactive reskilling policy and communications are required.

Quotes

Conclusions

  • It is sensible for Kazakhstan to bet on physical AI in applied pilots with high safety and efficiency value.
  • Regulation should combine support for innovation with data localization and ethical limitations on robot applications.
  • Linking university developments (tactile sensors, manipulators) with industrial use cases will accelerate commercialization.

Decisions Made

  • Launch industry pilots with robots in mining, energy, and agriculture with an emphasis on safety.
  • Initiate development of national cloud infrastructure for robotics with a data localization policy.
  • Create a cross-sector working group (government agencies, universities, business) to prepare a baseline regulation on robots and AI.
  • Organize a bridge program between school/student robotics and industry (internships, joint projects, grants).
  • Support R&D on key hardware components (actuators, energy efficiency, tactile sensors, and gripper systems).

Action Items

  • Identify 3–5 pilot sites (mines, pipelines, agro-enterprises) and technical specifications for robot tasks in 2025.
  • Audit robot data flows and prepare requirements for localization/anonymization and access for oversight.
  • Set up a grant competition for startups to solve applied tasks (inspection, delivery, care, agro) with co-financing.
  • Deploy a joint industry–NU laboratory on tactile robotics (AI Gripper) and pilot manipulation tasks.
  • Develop an educational track on physical AI (calculus, physics, mechatronics, simulation) for schools and universities.
  • Prepare a communication campaign about the benefits and safe deployment of robots, including measures for worker reskilling.
