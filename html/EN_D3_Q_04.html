AI and smart cities: human-centricity, air quality, and cultural heritage

Indira Makhmetova — Founder of Archica Congresses
Zhuldyz Saulebekova — Director of Almaty Air Initiative
Rikkardo Marini — Founder of Urban Living, Almaty
Bashaar Tarabai — Head of research projects
Stantsa — Independent artist

Annotation

The panel discussed how AI and AIoT are changing city governance and quality of life: from adaptive transport to air monitoring. Speakers argued for human-centricity and a “happy city” instead of technocentrism, as well as for the sovereignty of urban data. A separate emphasis was placed on the digitization of cultural heritage as a protection of identity amid climate risks and technological homogenization. Practical steps for Almaty were discussed: sensors, predictive analytics, and open dashboards.

Goals

  • Understand how AI and AIoT practically improve city management and quality of life
  • Define human-centric principles and metrics for smart projects (“happy city”)
  • Formulate an approach to urban data sovereignty and ethics, avoiding vendor monopolies
  • Determine how digitizing heritage strengthens the identity and resilience of cities
  • Assemble a set of actionable steps for Almaty (air quality, transport, open data)

Key Questions

  • How is AI already transforming city governance and improving urban quality of life?
  • How can we ensure that smart cities are not only technological but also human-centric and inclusive?
  • How can we digitize and protect cultural heritage without losing the identity of cities?
  • What fundamental processes and competencies must be in place before large-scale AI deployment?
  • How can citizens and communities participate in decision-making and change everyday practices?

Key Points

  • AI as the “brain” of the city and AIoT as the “body”: vision (sensors), thinking (analytics), action (automation) — the foundation of smart operations.
  • AIoT practices: adaptive traffic lights (Barcelona), safety and incident prediction (New York, Shenzhen), blockchain electricity trading for EVs (Shenzhen), energy consumption management (Amsterdam), smart bins (Singapore).
  • Almaty: severe air pollution and traffic jams are a single problem; a wide sensor network, dashboards, and predictive analytics are needed (wind, temperature, fires, pollen).
  • Human-centricity before technology: the goal is a “happy city”; if technology doesn’t make people happier and healthier, it has failed.
  • Data sovereignty: urban data should belong to citizens; it is unacceptable to give full control to a single vendor (risk of lock-in and inefficiency).
  • Critical use of AI: the tool is “dumb” without training; hallucinations and errors are possible. Authorship, verifiability, and professional accountability are needed.
  • Cultural heritage as the soul of the city: without understanding the past, a sustainable future is impossible. AI helps analyze risks (satellites, big data), but there is a risk of homogenization and loss of identity.
  • Climate challenges are already reshaping cities (example of Ghadames, Libya); solutions must take local context into account (example of Oman, rejection of skyscrapers in favor of traditions).
  • Art and data: the city as an interwoven collaborative space; artistic practices with sensors and visualizations reveal invisible layers of data and the ethical risks of “surveillance capitalism.”
  • Changing citizen behavior is part of the solution: walking, cycling, recycling, exerting pressure on authorities through manifestos, and demanding air quality as a human right.

Conclusions

  • AI and AIoT significantly expand the capabilities of urban management, but their effectiveness depends on human-centric design and the maturity of foundational processes.
  • Preserving identity through digitization and storytelling of heritage is a strategic necessity amid climate and technological challenges.
  • Data sovereignty and openness, rejection of single-vendor dependence, and citizen engagement are key to a sustainable and equitable digital transformation of the city.

Decisions Made

  • Adhere to a human-centric approach and the “happy city” metric when evaluating the effects of AI projects.
  • Enshrine the principle that urban data belongs to citizens; prevent transferring full control to a single vendor.
  • Expand the air quality sensor network in Almaty, and develop dashboards and predictive analytics (including for fires and pollen).
  • Launch programs for digital recording and storytelling of cultural heritage with the participation of communities and museums.

Action Items

  • Conduct an audit of basic city processes and data before automation; train teams to work with AI and verify results.
  • Develop a city manifesto (walking, cycling, emission reduction) and an air roadmap with measurable target indicators.
  • Launch a pilot of adaptive traffic lights on key corridors (e.g., Al-Farabi), taking into account pedestrian and traffic flows in real time.
  • Create a data sovereignty policy: a registry of sensors, open APIs, access levels, and rules for reinvesting data revenues back into the city.
  • Organize a public behavior-change program: campaigns for walking/cycling, separate waste collection, and voluntary avoidance of unnecessary trips.
  • Together with Iconem and museums, launch a pilot for 3D scanning of heritage sites and creating digital exhibitions with AI storytelling.
