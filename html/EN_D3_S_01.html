How to Raise an 'Elon Musk': AI, technocracy, and the effectiveness of school education

Almas Ordabayev — CEO, Quantum School
Rauan Kenzhekhan — Founder and CEO, Bilim Media Group
Madi Kargin — Director, BI Education
Dana Moldakulova — FINNOVATION co-founder, Project Lead, KAIST KAZAKHSTAN
Nurmukhammed Dosybayev — Founder of Hyperion School and Astana Garden School

Annotation

The panel discussed: 1) how to safely and usefully introduce AI in schools without destroying the 'teacher–student' connection, 2) teacher training and proctoring standards as the key to quality and academic integrity, 3) a technocratic development model using Korea as an example with a focus on research universities and the 'university–industry' link, 4) student motivation (especially boys), differentiated methods, and measuring well-being and functional literacy.

Goals

  • Within 12–18 months, create and implement a framework for AI ethics and safe use in schools (Kazakh/Russian content and academic integrity policy).
  • Scale teacher training in AI competencies, proctoring, and plagiarism checking through a peer-to-peer model and partnerships with EdTech companies.
  • Annually measure functional literacy and NPS of students/parents in all partner schools and use the data for management decisions.
  • Launch pilot 'school–industry' research laboratories and prepare a roadmap toward opening KAS/KAIST Kazakhstan (2026 plan).
  • Deploy motivation programs and differentiated methods (including for boys and inclusion), integrating SEL and well-being practices.

Key Questions

  • How to ensure that AI enhances learning and the 'teacher–student' connection rather than replacing them?
  • What proctoring and plagiarism-checking standards are applicable to schools and how can they be scaled?
  • How to balance the internationalization of education with national interests and the development of Kazakh scientific content?
  • Where to start the early technologization of education (preschool/elementary) to cultivate a research mindset?
  • How to increase student motivation and responsibility (especially for boys) through differentiated pedagogy and a culture of big goals?

Key Points

  • AI in education requires clear usage ethics: the tool should help the teacher design learning and not provide ready-made answers to the student.
  • The critical foundation is staff preparation: without systemic upskilling of teachers and administrators, technologies do not deliver results.
  • 80% of EdTech startups fail due to weak data, a wrong product course, and a lack of trained personnel.
  • Functional literacy and critical thinking are protection against the 'hallucinations' of generative AI; measurements should be regular (at least once a year).
  • NPS and comments from students/parents are fast indicators of problems, especially in the 'middle grades' (grades 7–8), where new needs are formed.
  • Proctoring and anti-plagiarism standards must be implemented together with staff training and small pilots.
  • Korean technocracy model: reliance on technological universities and laboratories, close ties with industry, applied science, and commercialization.
  • The KAS/KAIST Kazakhstan research university is planned as industry-oriented, with laboratories for company orders (sensors/chips, etc.).
  • Balance of internationalization and national identity: new standards are welcome, but priority goes to national interests and the development of the Kazakh language in science.
  • Motivation of boys requires setting high expectations, experiences of overcoming difficulties, and differentiated methods; education is work, not play.

Conclusions

  • Without systemic teacher training and academic integrity norms, AI implementation will not yield a sustainable effect.
  • A technocratic trajectory through research universities and the 'university–industry' link is applicable in Kazakhstan and can become an economic driver.
  • Regular metrics (functional literacy, NPS, well-being) should drive decisions at the level of schools and networks.
  • Student motivation and responsibility, especially for boys, are strengthened through differentiation, high expectations, and a culture of overcoming.

Decisions Made

  • Decided to scale teacher training in AI competencies and proctoring via a peer-to-peer model with the participation of EdTech partners.
  • Decided to measure functional literacy and NPS annually in all partner schools and use the findings to adjust programs.
  • Decided to launch pilots of an AI assistant for teachers (without issuing ready-made answers to students) in a number of schools.
  • Decided to form a working group to develop a framework for AI ethics and academic integrity with an emphasis on the Kazakh language and local content.
  • Decided to start preparing industry-oriented 'school–industry' laboratories as a pre-stage to launching KAS/KAIST Kazakhstan (plan-2026).
  • Decided to implement SEL and well-being programs with targeted support for the middle grades (grades 7–8) and differentiated methods by gender and inclusion.

Action Items

  • Develop training modules and manuals on AI for teachers; conduct waves of p2p training (groups of 15–20 people) across school networks.
  • Develop and approve standards for proctoring, anti-plagiarism, and verification protocols for tests/olympiads.
  • Roll out an annual assessment cycle: functional literacy (PISA-like), NPS, and SEL/well-being questionnaires; set up analytics for comments.
  • Launch a pilot of a teacher AI assistant together with a technology partner; define impact metrics (lesson quality, teacher workload).
  • Create 2–3 pilot 'school–industry' laboratories (sensors/IoT/chip design) with the participation of 5–10 client companies.
  • Prepare a roadmap for KAS/KAIST Kazakhstan: partnerships with Korean universities, recruitment of professors, a list of laboratories, and commercialization KPIs.
  • Develop a motivation program for boys: high expectations, mentoring, a system of trials/competitions, differentiated methods.
  • Expand the network of olympiads and internal tournaments to train 'wins–losses' and resilience, integrating principles of academic integrity.
