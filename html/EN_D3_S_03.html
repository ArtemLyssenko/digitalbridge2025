EdTech and AI: creating global educational platforms of the future

Laura — moderator of the Digital Bridge panel session
Askar Bolat — CEO of Qalan
Dmitrii Gavdur — CEO of Lerna
Dastan Bekmurza — Secretary General of the Federation of Competitive Programming of Kazakhstan; CEO of Kodi Play
Asem Tazhiyeva — Founder of the Public Foundation Ozim Platform, Head of Ozim Solutions Ltd

Annotation

The discussion focuses on the role of AI in EdTech: from risks of politicization and questions of data sovereignty to practical personalization of learning and inclusion. The speakers divided AI applications into control/analytics, tools for teachers, and learner augmentation, emphasizing the need for real, not 'hype' AI assistants. They discussed gamification and the myth of a 'short attention span', as well as the balance between openness of technology and regulation to protect sensitive data.

Goals

  • Identify effective and safe scenarios for using AI in education for students, teachers, and management systems.
  • Form an approach to personalized learning that accounts for inclusion and the protection of sensitive data.
  • Define the boundaries of digital hygiene and data sovereignty in Kazakhstan's EdTech ecosystem.
  • Determine the priorities for EdTech transformation: gamification, outcome-based learning, and high-quality AI assistants.

Key Questions

  • Will sovereign AI become a tool of political control in education?
  • Will artificial intelligence replace teachers and schools, and what will happen to classic EdTech then?
  • How do we teach children in the 'TikTok era': is attention span really falling, and does gamification help?
  • Who is responsible for inclusion when implementing AI: the state, developers, schools, or educators?
  • Where is the line between personalization and data privacy, and how can countries with 'data poverty' compete?

Key Points

  • AI in EdTech has three key directions: administrative control and reporting; tools to increase teacher efficiency; personal assistants to augment the learner.
  • For adult EdTech, the 'pre-recorded video' format is becoming a thing of the past: schools that create practical AI assistants, boost engagement, and lead the student to a measurable outcome will survive.
  • Gamification and intrinsic/extrinsic motivation are key to retaining attention; personalized AI mentors cover up to 80% of a teacher's tasks.
  • The myth of a declining attention span: modern children are not weaker — they require different formats; Olympiad practice confirms concentration with the right methodology.
  • School is not only knowledge but also a social environment: even with individualization, it is important to preserve skills of empathy, communication, and teamwork.
  • Inclusion = personalization for everyone: we need student profiles, collection and analysis of sensitive data with clear rules for protection and de-identification.
  • Data sovereignty and infrastructure: local models (KZLM), servers and supercomputers in the РК, in parallel — exchange of de-identified datasets.
  • Digital hygiene is a shared responsibility: users should share data consciously; developers and government agencies should ensure security.
  • Development strategy: first stimulate technological growth (by analogy with fintech), then regulate flexibly, avoiding early overregulation.
  • Global cooperation is more important than protectionism for small markets: openness to technologies and partnerships accelerates progress and reduces the gap.

Quotes

“Gamification is the main trend in EdTech, and personalized AI mentors already replace up to 80% of a teacher's functions.” — Askar Bolat
“We should not divide children into 'neurotypical' and 'non-neurotypical': everyone has their own needs, and personalization requires sensitive data.” — Asem Tazhiyeva

Conclusions

  • AI does not cancel the role of the teacher and the school but redistributes emphasis: routine to machines, leadership, creativity, and social skills to humans.
  • Kazakhstan should develop local AI infrastructure and privacy standards while remaining open to global technologies and the exchange of de-identified data.
  • Personalization and inclusion require systematic collection of student profiles and secure processing of sensitive data.
  • EdTech effectiveness is determined not by 'hype' but by measurable student outcomes and sustainable motivation (gamification, assistants, journey support).

Decisions Made

  • Focus on creating practical AI assistants for students and teachers instead of declarative 'AI branding'.
  • Support the development of local models and infrastructure (e.g., KZLM) with priority on de-identification and sovereign data storage.
  • Begin systematic collection and maintenance of learner profiles for personalization in compliance with privacy requirements.
  • Promote digital hygiene among users and educators as a fundamental skill for working with AI tools.

Action Items

  • Launch pilots of gamified AI tutors in schools and on supplementary education platforms with measurement of impact on course completion.
  • Develop industry standards for de-identification and storage of sensitive data (jointly with regulators and EdTech companies).
  • Create an AI upskilling program for educators (collection and analysis of learning tracks, working with assistants, digital hygiene).
  • Organize a cross-sector consortium (government agencies–business–universities–employers) for sharing best practices and datasets.
  • Deploy local computing capacities and sandboxes for safe testing of educational models.
  • Publish transparent ethical guidelines on inclusive design of AI products and procedures for informed consent.
