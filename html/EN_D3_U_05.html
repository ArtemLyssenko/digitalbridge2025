Annotation

The panel discussed the current state of the comics and animation scene in Kazakhstan: from self-publishing and personal brands to the shortage of a formalized industry. The participants emphasized the role of digital infrastructure and social networks in rapid growth, the search for a 'cultural code' beyond clichés about folklore alone, and export potential. They outlined practical entry routes (portfolio, agencies, Behance) and cautiously assessed the role of AI: useful for concepts, but harmful to a long-term brand.

Goals

  • Assess the current state and prospects of the comics and animation industry in Kazakhstan
  • Identify working go-to-market and export models (self-publishing, platforms, agencies)
  • Define elements of the Kazakhstani 'cultural code' in content beyond folklore
  • Share practical advice for newcomers: portfolio, media presence, community
  • Discuss appropriate scenarios for applying AI in content production

Key Questions

  • Should growth rely only on folklore, or are there other thematic directions?
  • How to popularize comics and animation in Kazakhstan and take them to export?
  • What are the real entry paths into the profession: where to find first clients and projects?
  • How can an introverted artist find commissions and build an online presence?
  • Does AI help create animation: when is it appropriate and what tools to use?

Key Points

  • The Kazakhstani scene is at an early stage: there are strong individual cases, but there is little formalized industry and stable demand.
  • Digital infrastructure (social networks, platforms, payments) shortens the path to the audience and distribution; an author's personal brand directly affects sales.
  • Folklore is only one pillar; the cultural code can be revealed through contemporaneity: post-Soviet 2000s aesthetics, urban context, linguistic agenda, etc.
  • Self-publishing is viable: solo production of manga/comics allows testing plots and accumulating a fanbase (example: sales of «Бескунар», the release of «Мирген»).
  • Comics as a gateway to major screen adaptation: a 'publish first, adapt later' model reduces risk (Japanese-Korean school).
  • Effective channels for commissions: portfolios on Behance/ArtStation/Instagram, word of mouth, agencies and production studios, case projects (TikTok Live, VFX for cinema).
  • Community and education boost talent inflow: courses, school programs in digital art, peer referrals and mentorship.
  • Introverts can scale through volume and quality of content: 'when there's a lot of material — you can't be ignored.'
  • AI is helpful in specific spots (references, quick concepts), but as 'fast-food content' for brands it harms long-term audience trust; better to invest in handcrafted quality.
  • Export is a must: the local market is small, but interest in comics and animation is growing; it's important to show up on external platforms.

Conclusions

  • Growth is possible through a combination of self-publishing, a strong portfolio, and an export orientation, supported by community and education.
  • AI should be used as an auxiliary tool rather than a substitute for a creative hand if the goal is a long-term brand and audience trust.

Decisions Made

  • Launch the creator platform Perfect Q (Weekly Perfect) for publishing Kazakhstani manga/comics and collecting statistics for subsequent adaptation.
  • Continue educational initiatives: courses in animation/illustration and school programs in digital art.
  • Focus on export: systematically upload portfolios to international platforms and work with overseas agencies/brands.
  • Develop the local 'cultural code' in new genres (melodrama, horror, post-apocalyptic aesthetics) alongside folklore.
  • Expand the community and peer recommendations to accelerate new authors' entry into projects.
  • Explore integrations with mass digital services (carrier apps, dailies) to attract a broad audience to reading comics.
