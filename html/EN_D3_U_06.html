Gamedev in Uzbekistan

Akmal Salikhov — Director of Business Development, East Games

Annotation

The session showed how in recent years the gamedev ecosystem in Uzbekistan has taken shape: tax incentives and exports, growth in the number of studios, and an indie uptick. The results of a survey of indie teams (composition, genres, engines, pain points) were presented and gaps in last‑mile financing and education were discussed. Special emphasis was placed on practical cases and trends: profitable genres, a 2D renaissance, as well as the synergy of AI (Claude MCP) and Unity to accelerate prototyping and production.

Goals

  • Provide an overview of the state of Uzbekistan’s gamedev and its export potential against the backdrop of reforms and incentives
  • Identify key barriers for indie teams: financing, education, visibility
  • Share indie survey data and practical recommendations on genres, engines, and production
  • Show the capabilities of AI tools (Claude MCP + Unity) for small teams
  • Consolidate the community around an open report and further joint steps

Key Questions

  • Why does it make sense to build a career and studios in Uzbekistan instead of relocating?
  • How can indies close the 'last mile' of financing and correctly assess a project for investors?
  • Which genres and platforms are more rational for indies today (and what to avoid)?
  • What government support measures and educational initiatives does Uzbekistan’s gamedev need?
  • How do AI (Claude MCP) and Unity change prototyping efficiency and team size?

Key Points

  • Demographics and safety create the foundation: 38 million population, 55% youth; a multinational environment and affordable daily life.
  • Government reforms and IT Park Uzbekistan incentives substantially reduce the payroll burden, which attracts exporters.
  • The gamedev sector is visible in IT exports: according to IT Park — about 600 employed; on GameDevMap for Tashkent — ~12 companies; total residents with the activity type 'games' — around 27 (noisy data).
  • East Games is the first large studio with an office 'not in a basement'; content outsourcing chains are forming for major titles (example: the contribution of a Tashkent team to World of Warships).
  • Survey of ~50 indie teams: average team size — 3 people; profiles are diverse, but there is an oversupply of 'self‑declared' game designers; there are no women in 70% of teams.
  • Indie genre plans diverge from market monetization: few city‑builders, an excess of platformers; the fact of platformer oversaturation on Steam is ignored.
  • Engines: Unity dominates; Unreal is unpopular; stylized 3D prevails; the potential of modern 2D is underestimated (cases like Dave the Diver).
  • Main release barriers: lack of money for the final phase, team breakups, underestimation of scope and a shortage of time/motivation; critical 'bridge' funding of $5–10k for polishing.
  • Education and visibility are weak spots: no specialized gamedev grants and schools in Uzbek; Russian‑language content is not accessible to everyone.
  • Compensation: in large studios, a premium to market and release bonuses; for indies — lower fixed pay but possible options; a benchmark for a junior in Tashkent is ~$400–600.

Conclusions

  • Uzbekistan’s gamedev ecosystem has formed and is growing thanks to incentives and exports, but it runs into a shortage of specialized education and targeted funding for indies.
  • The market needs 'last‑mile' instruments (micro‑grants, seed checks) and genre‑market discipline among indies (fewer platformers, more economically sustainable genres).
  • AI (Claude MCP) paired with Unity radically accelerates prototyping, reducing team size and the cost of experiments.
