AI cinema and authorial expression: the case study of the film Porcelain Abuse and the experience of Yuldus Bakhtiozina

Yuldus Bakhtiozina — Director, artist
Moderator (name not specified) — Digital Bridge session host

Annotation

A conversation with artist and director Yuldus Bakhtiozina about how to use AI to create not only visual effects but also a full-fledged narrative. Her AI-generated film Porcelain Abuse is presented — the first in the Russian Federation to be registered by the Ministry of Culture and released in wide distribution, a «Короче» laureate. Discussed: authorial style in the era of generative models, practical production aspects (time, budget, tools), and ethical-legal as well as gender issues in AI creativity.

Goals

  • Show that AI is capable of creating an authorial narrative and emotion, not just short visual clips
  • Break down the practice of producing an AI film: script, generations, editing, budget, and timing
  • Discuss preserving an authorial style and the artist’s identity in the digital era
  • Highlight ethical and legal aspects (portrayals, likeness, copyrights) in AI cinema
  • Give recommendations to young directors and artists on working with neural networks and phygital approaches

Key Questions

  • How do you turn AI generations into a coherent cinematic narrative rather than a set of flashy shots?
  • What are the timelines, process, and budget of a real AI film, and where are the main bottlenecks?
  • How can an artist preserve authorial identity and uniqueness given mass access to AI tools?
  • Is AI ready for feature-length cinema, and where do human limitations lie (perseverance, editing, selection)?
  • How to handle copyright and similarity to famous characters/faces in AI visuals?

Key Points

  • Porcelain Abuse is Yuldus’s AI-generated film, the first in the Russian Federation to be registered by the Ministry of Culture and released in wide distribution; it received the Best Directing award at the «Короче» festival.
  • AI can create not just 'pictures' but a full-fledged authorial statement and emotion; the key is the script and directing.
  • Production case: about 21 days of 8-hour generations, stretched over ~3.5 months; a budget of around €600; a significant share of time went to selection, editing, and 'polishing' shots.
  • The film’s themes are the race for perfection, the objectification of women, and the illusion of uniqueness; porcelain dolls serve as a metaphor for vulnerability and 'flaws'.
  • Speaker’s position: not 'anti-digital' but for distinguishing experiences — physical (transformation) and visual (avatar/AI); a phygital format is possible.
  • AI’s accessibility democratizes entry into the industry (months versus decades of craft practice) but intensifies competition and complicates quality selection — fears are often tied to the risk of ending up 'on the sidelines'.
  • Yuldus’s next project is a hybrid film combining live action and AI generations to cover VFX tasks with limited budgets.
  • Training: Yuldus runs infrequent international courses and webinars; the focus is on 'tuning the brain' for working with neural networks and prompt formulas.
  • Law and ethics: artistic resemblance is permissible without 100% copying of a specific image; IP issues require attentiveness.
  • Feature-length AI cinema is technically possible; the main barrier is human resources (time, selection, editing), not model readiness.

Conclusions

  • AI is already applicable to auteur cinema and can sustain a complex narrative given strong writing and directing.
  • Tool accessibility will increase competition; preserving a unique style and combining physical and digital experience become key.
  • Hybrid (live action + AI) production is a promising path for independent directors with limited budgets.
